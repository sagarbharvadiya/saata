I have been working with some members of the SAATA community for many years now, and it has been important personally and professionally to have these connections in my life. However, meeting online is not the same as meeting in person. This was my first visit to Bangalore and the experience I had of witnessing the diversity of life in the city and surrounding countryside will be with me and be a part of me as I journey on in life.

The conference itself was lively, stimulating, and inspiring. It was so good to see the diversity of practice within the SAATA community and the willingness to engage with the keynotes, the discussions, and the workshops. I had a real sense of how the community is developing its own ideas and authority in thinking about Transactional Analysis and the varied ways in which it can work. To hear of SAATA’s new MHTAP and DTAP was also affirmative of the community making their qualifications socially relevant to context.

I was very affected by the willingness of people to explore social and political challenges and the ways in which strengths and vulnerabilities manifest with identities both of privilege and discrimination. Many know these are subjects close to my heart and can be very difficult to talk about, given the fear of being ostracised in groups. The interest and growing motivation to learn about group relations and group analysis was most encouraging and I saw some deep reflective processes at work. Indeed, the diversity of lives lived in India and the rich resources in this community can only continue to grow and prosper, given the levels of motivation and commitment that I saw.

I left Bangalore on Tuesday 27th September after nearly a week there. At security, the Indian immigration officer asked me if I was Irish. I was taken aback…”Well, no,” I said, “I come from England.” He pointed to “Northern Ireland '' on my passport and if he hadn’t been smiling, I might well have had a paranoid moment that someone had been spying on me. So, for the second time in India, I had the opportunity to explain the situation in Northern Ireland and its relationship with the UK. Perhaps just a strange coincidence, perhaps synchronicity.

I wish you all well as you go forward to keep claiming your way of thinking about Transactional Analysis and your way of applying the theory in practice. I also look forward to enjoying my ongoing connections with your community.


Karen Shireen Minikin
Keynote speaker at SAATA Conference 2023
